Module A

Research Design & Theory

Before you touch the software, we must define the "Blueprint" of your research. If you get this wrong, your SmartPLS results will be invalid, no matter how good the numbers look.

1. Construct Conceptualization

The most common mistake beginners make is mixing up Reflective and Formative constructs. SmartPLS treats them very differently mathematically.

Reflective vs. Formative: The "Chicken or the Egg?"

Ask yourself: "Does the construct cause the indicators, or do the indicators cause the construct?"

1. Reflective (Most Common)

The Construct (Egg) exists first, and it causes the answers (Chickens).
Example: "Customer Satisfaction". You are satisfied, therefore you agree with the questions.

2. Formative (Index)

The Indicators (Ingredients) combine to create the Construct (Meal).
Example: "Socioeconomic Status". Income + Education + Job = SES. High income doesn't mean high SES without education.

The 4-Question Decision Rule (Jarvis et al.)

Ask these 4 questions. If you answer "No" to any, your construct is likely Formative.

  1. Do the indicators share a common theme?
  2. Are the indicators interchangeable?
  3. Would dropping an indicator not change the conceptual domain of the construct?
  4. Do indicators have similar antecedents and consequences?
Default Mode
If unsure, assume Reflective. 90% of studies use Reflective.
Reflective

2. Questionnaire & Scale Design

Garbage in, garbage out. Your data must be measured properly to yield valid PLS results.

The Likert Scale

In PLS-SEM, we generally treat Likert scales as Interval Data (quasi-metric).

Recommended
7-Point or 5-Point scales are standard. Avoid 3-point scales (not enough variance).
5 or 7 Points

Example 7-Point Scale:

1. Strongly Disagree ... 4. Neutral ... 7. Strongly Agree

Where do I get the questions?

Never invent your own questions unless you have to.

  • Search Google Scholar for "[Your Construct] measurement scale".
  • Look for seminal papers (e.g., "Davis (1989) for Technology Acceptance").
  • Adapt, don't copy: Change wording slightly to fit your context (e.g., "The website" -> "The Mobile App").

"Using established scales ensures your Discriminant Validity (later steps) will actually work."

3. Hypothesis Formulation

A hypothesis is a predictive statement about the relationship between two variables.

The Three Parts of a Hypothesis:

  1. Variable 1 (Independent): The cause.
  2. Variable 2 (Dependent): The effect.
  3. Direction: Positive (+) or Negative (-).

Standard Academic Notation

Reviewers expect specific notation.

H1: Website Quality has a positive significant effect on Customer Satisfaction.

Mathematical Notation:
Website Quality → Customer Satisfaction (+)

In SmartPLS, this becomes an arrow pointing from "Website Quality" to "Customer Satisfaction".
