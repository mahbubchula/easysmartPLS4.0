Module E

Advanced Analysis

Go beyond basic hypothesis testing. This module covers Prediction (PLSpredict), Management Priorities (IPMA), and Configurational Logic (NCA & fsQCA).

1. Predictive Assessment (PLSpredict)

PLS-SEM is great for prediction. PLSpredict is the modern standard to prove your model works on data it hasn't seen.

In-Sample vs. Out-of-Sample

R² tells you how well the model explains the data you already have.

PLSpredict splits your data into a training sample and a holdout sample. It builds the model on the training data and then tries to predict the values in the holdout sample.

Data Split: 90% (Training) | 10% (Holdout)
SmartPLS predicts Holdout values using Training model.

RMSE & MAE

We look at the prediction error. Lower is better.

  • RMSE: Root Mean Squared Error (Penalizes large errors).
  • MAE: Mean Absolute Error (Easier to interpret).

Benchmark: LM vs. PLS

SmartPLS compares the PLS prediction error against a simple Linear Regression (LM) benchmark.

Good Prediction
PLS prediction error (RMSE/MAE) is lower than Linear Regression.
PLS < LM

Also check Q²_predict. It should be > 0 for the key endogenous constructs.

Running PLSpredict

  1. Go to Calculate -> PLSpredict.
  2. Select the number of folds (usually 10) and repetitions.
  3. Select the prediction target (endogenous constructs).
  4. Click Start Analysis.
  5. Review the table at the bottom: Prediction Statistics.

2. IPMA (Importance-Performance Map Analysis)

Standard PLS tells you "Is X important?". IPMA tells you "X is important, but are we doing it well?"

Two Dimensions

IPMA creates a scatterplot for your target construct:

  • X-Axis (Performance): The average latent variable scores (0 to 100). High means customers are satisfied with X.
  • Y-Axis (Importance): The total effects of X on the target. High means X strongly drives the target.
High Importance / Low Performance = PRIORITIZE THIS!

Management Implications

Q1: High Importance / High Performance

"Keep up the good work." These are your strengths.

Q2: High Importance / Low Performance

Action Area. This drives success but you are failing at it.

Q3: Low Importance / Low Performance

"Ignore." Doesn't matter much.

Q4: Low Importance / High Performance

"Possible Overkill." You are wasting resources here.

3. Necessary Condition Analysis (NCA)

Regression (PLS) tells you if X contributes to Y. NCA tells you if X is required for Y.

The "Oxygen" Analogy

Sufficient (PLS): Eating cake makes you happy. (If you eat it, you are happy. If you don't eat it, you might still be happy from something else).

Necessary (NCA): Oxygen is necessary to live. Without it, you die. Having it doesn't guarantee a long life (you might get hit by a bus), but you MUST have it.

The Ceiling Line

NCA draws a "Ceiling" (a line) across the top-left of your scatterplot.

No data points can exist above the ceiling (because it's logically impossible).

Logic: If the condition (X) is low, the outcome (Y) CANNOT be high.

Effect Size (d) and Bottlenecks

Necessary Effect Size (d)
Small (0.1), Medium (0.3), Large (0.5). If p < 0.05, the condition is necessary.
d ≥ 0.1

Bottleneck Analysis: What is the minimum level of X required to achieve a desired level of Y (e.g., 80% Satisfaction)?

4. fsQCA (Configurational Logic)

Instead of looking at variables one-by-one (net effects), fsQCA looks at combinations of conditions (recipes) that lead to an outcome.

Equifinality

PLS assumes one path fits everyone. fsQCA assumes there are multiple paths (recipes) to the same outcome.

Example:

  • Recipe 1: High Price * High Quality = Loyalty
  • Recipe 2: Low Price * Convenience = Loyalty

Both lead to Loyalty, but through different configurations.

The Truth Table Algorithm

SmartPLS integrates fsQCA (using the R package). It analyzes the data to identify these recipes.

Thresholds

Consistency
Does the recipe consistently produce the outcome?
> 0.75 (0.80 strict)
Coverage
How much of the outcome does this recipe explain?
Higher is better
