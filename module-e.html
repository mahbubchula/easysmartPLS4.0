Module E

Advanced Analysis in PLS-SEM

This module extends beyond basic hypothesis testing. We examine prediction, mediation, moderation, managerial relevance, necessity logic, and configurational pathways.

0. When Do You Need Advanced Analysis?

  • When reviewers ask: “Is your model predictive?”
  • When theory suggests indirect or conditional effects
  • When managerial or policy implications are required
  • When linear net-effects are insufficient
Key Idea
Advanced analysis does not replace structural results; it deepens interpretation.

1. Predictive Assessment (PLSpredict)

Why PLSpredict?

R² and Q² evaluate explanatory power. PLSpredict evaluates true out-of-sample prediction.

Explanatory model: “Does X explain Y?” Predictive model: “Can X predict future Y?”

Evaluation Criteria

  • RMSE / MAE: Lower values indicate better prediction
  • Benchmark: PLS vs. Linear Model (LM)
  • Q²predict: Must be > 0
Strong Prediction
PLS error < LM error
PLS < LM

2. Mediation Analysis

Mediation explains how or why an independent variable influences an outcome.

Basic Mediation Structure

X → M → Y (Indirect effect: X → M × M → Y)

Types of Mediation

  • Full mediation: Direct effect becomes insignificant
  • Partial mediation: Both direct and indirect effects significant
Decision Rule
Use bootstrapped confidence intervals for indirect effects.

3. Moderation Analysis

Moderation answers: “When or for whom does X affect Y?”

Moderation Logic

Effect of X on Y depends on Z (X × Z → Y)

Implementation in SmartPLS

  • Product Indicator (default)
  • Two-Stage approach (recommended for complex models)
Interpretation
A significant interaction term confirms moderation.

4. Importance–Performance Map Analysis (IPMA)

IPMA translates statistical results into actionable managerial or policy insights.

Two Dimensions

  • Importance: Total effects on target construct
  • Performance: Average latent variable scores
High Importance + Low Performance = Immediate priority

IPMA is especially powerful in marketing, service quality, transport policy, and sustainability research.

5. Necessary Condition Analysis (NCA)

NCA identifies conditions that must be present for an outcome to occur at all.

Key Difference

  • PLS: Sufficient conditions
  • NCA: Necessary conditions
Effect Size (d)
Necessary condition confirmed
d ≥ 0.10

Bottleneck analysis shows the minimum level of X required to achieve a given level of Y.

6. fsQCA (Configurational Analysis)

fsQCA is based on set theory and recognizes equifinality (multiple pathways to the same outcome).

Why fsQCA?

  • Asymmetric causality
  • Combinations matter more than single variables
High Quality * High Trust → Adoption Low Price * High Convenience → Adoption
Consistency
Recipe reliability
≥ 0.80
